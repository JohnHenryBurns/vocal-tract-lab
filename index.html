Vocal Tract Lab

I wanted a goal cry.

I built a three-sided football game with my kids. Three teams, one ball, no permanent friends. It narrates its own matches, so it needed someone to shout GOOOOAAALLL when a goal goes in.

A sound file would have worked. But then it's the same shout every time, and kids notice that by the third match. I wanted the computer to actually make the sound.

First attempt was filters and math faking a voice. Nine rounds of it. Along the way it sounded like a ghost, then a sasquatch, then a cow, then a Spanish speaker, then a sheep. Each of those turned out to be pointing at something genuinely broken, so they were useful. It still never got close.

So we stopped faking it and built the throat.

That's what this is. It isn't done. Press Play and you'll hear that. But it's real physics now instead of a guess, and it's getting closer.

Try it: type a word and press ▶. That is the whole thing. Everything else — other voices, the tongue, the tuning — is optional.

Five controls.

Along the bottom: ∿ sustains whatever shape the tract is in — one sound, held, for as long as you like. Which sound that is depends on what you last touched: tap a phoneme and it is that one, move the articulator sliders and it is whatever the tube now makes. A nasal shape gives you a hum; a stop gives you silence, because a sealed tube has nothing to radiate. ▶ is the other thing entirely — a whole word, articulated in sequence. One is a note, the other is a melody. The voice picker changes who is talking, Word is where a word comes from, and Advanced holds the articulators, the timing, the tract length and the Lab.

Play word speaks the word you have built. It opens on goal. While it is speaking, that button becomes Stop.

The Voice list is not a set of effects. Each entry changes the length of the tube along with the source: a woman's tract is about 14.7 cm against a man's 17.5, and that shortening is what lifts every formant — pitch alone will not do it. Helium is the same idea arriving by another route: the gas carries sound faster, so the same voice at the same pitch rings a tube that is acoustically half the size. You can hear source and filter come apart.

Word is where a word comes from. Type one and it is sounded out for you; tap the sounds it chose and a phoneme keyboard opens, with a backspace, so you can correct it the way you would correct a spelling. New word starts clean. The timing sliders set how long the word takes and how much the first vowel is stretched.

Tract opens the tongue and voice controls. Tongue position slides the narrowing from the throat toward the lips, constriction sets how tight it is, and lip aperture rounds or spreads the mouth. Between those three you can reach most of the vowel space by hand.

Lab is where the tuning happens. It plays you two versions of the voice and you keep the better one. Do that ten times and it converges somewhere you would not have found by dragging sliders. If both sound wrong, say so — it will search somewhere else. A good voice can be copied out as a 20-character seed and pasted back later.

One thing worth knowing: a voice and a word are separate. Tune a voice on goal and it will speak bulldog the same way, without retuning.

Things it can already say: goal, gold, ball, bulldog, dad, good, bird. Anything built from vowels, /l/, and the stops b d g.

A tube decides the sound.

Blow across the top of a bottle and you get a note. Change the bottle and the note changes. The shape of the space inside is what decides it.

Your mouth works the same way. Your vocal cords make a buzz, and the tube above them turns that buzz into a vowel. Move your tongue and you change the shape of the tube. That is most of what a vowel is.

So the controls here are not abstract. They are a jaw, a tongue body, a tongue tip and lips — six numbers shared by every sound. That constraint matters: a tongue cannot be in two places, and lips are not part of it, so no setting can produce a shape a real mouth could not make.

This page is that tube, simulated. It is cut into 44 short segments, and sound bounces around inside it the way it does in your own head. Nothing here is a recording, and nothing here knows what a vowel is. It only knows the shape of the pipe.

Where the shapes came from

In 1952 Peterson and Barney measured the resonances of thousands of spoken vowels, and their numbers are still the standard reference. We searched over tube shapes until the simulation matched those measurements.

The interesting part is what fell out of that search. Nobody gave it any phonetics, but it independently found that /o/ needs a narrowing at the back of the throat plus rounded lips, and /ɑ/ a narrowing low in the throat with the jaw open. That is exactly what those vowels are.

Under the hood

The model is a Kelly–Lochbaum digital waveguide (1962). The tract is a chain of cylindrical sections carrying two counter-propagating travelling waves. Wherever the cross-sectional area changes, part of each wave reflects:

r = (A₁ − A₂) / (A₁ + A₂)

That is the whole model. There are no formant filters in the code. The resonances are standing waves, and each cylinder in the 3D view is lit by the wave energy actually present in that section, so you are watching the pressure pattern that is a formant.

The /l/ needs more than one tube. A lateral splits the airflow around the tongue and leaves a closed pocket, which puts a zero in the transfer function — and a single unbranched tube is an all-pole system that cannot produce one. So the tract carries a side branch, coupled through a three-port scattering junction:

pj = 2·Σu⁺ / ΣA  ·  u⁻ᵢ = Aᵢ·pj − u⁺ᵢ

which reduces exactly to the two-port form when the branch is shut, so it cannot disturb the tract it taps into. With the lateral re-solved around it, the engine measures a −33.7 dB notch at 1950 Hz. The same mechanism with an open far end at the velum gives the nasals.

The same algorithm plucks a string

Anyone who has built synthesizers will recognise the machinery. Karplus–Strong (1983) — a delay line with a loss filter in its feedback path, kicked with a burst of noise — is the standard way to synthesize a plucked string, and it is the same idea: sound in a one-dimensional medium is two travelling waves, so you simulate it by delaying and filtering rather than by solving anything.

Kelly and Lochbaum got there first, in 1962, with the vocal tract. Julius O. Smith later formalised the whole family as digital waveguide synthesis and showed that Karplus–Strong is a waveguide with the simplifications a string allows.

The difference is geometry. A string is uniform, so nothing scatters along its length and a single delay loop is enough. A vocal tract changes cross-section constantly, so it needs a scattering junction at every boundary — which is why this model carries 44 of them.

You can watch the two collapse into each other. Make every section of this tube the same diameter and every reflection coefficient goes to zero, leaving a plain delay loop. Then only the ends decide what it is:

Odd harmonics only is a tube stopped at one end — a clarinet, a throat. The full harmonic series is a string fixed at both ends. Same code, two numbers changed.

What is still missing

Nasals, voiceless stops and fricatives. And the glottal source is a Rosenberg pulse, which models speech — a shout is pressed phonation, not merely loud speech, so replacing it with the LF model is the next real step.
