Vocal Tract Lab

A throat you can play with.

Blow across the top of a bottle and you get a note. Change the bottle and the note changes. The shape of the space inside is what decides the sound.

Your mouth works the same way. Your vocal cords make a buzz, and the tube above them — throat, mouth, lips — turns that buzz into a vowel. Move your tongue and you change the shape of the tube. That is all a vowel really is.

This page is that tube, simulated. The 3D shape is a model of a human vocal tract cut into 44 short segments, and sound bounces around inside it the way it does in your own head. Nothing here is a recording. Nothing here even knows what a vowel is. It only knows the shape of the pipe, and the vowels come out on their own.

Try it: press Play word. Then open Word and tap different sounds to build your own.

Why a football game needed a throat

It started as a goal cry. A three-sided football simulator needed a stadium GOOOOAAALLL, and rather than ship a sound file we tried to build one from scratch. Nine attempts later it had sounded — in this order — like a ghost, a sasquatch, a cow, a Spanish speaker and a sheep. Each of those turned out to point at a real, specific fault. We gave up on that approach and modelled the tube instead.

The controls

Hold gives voice to whatever shape the tract is in right now, so you hear one sustained vowel. Play word speaks the word you have built; it starts as g o ɑ l. Word opens the sounds and the word. Tract opens the tongue and voice controls. Lab plays you two versions and lets you keep the better one, over and over, until the voice is what you want.

Where the shapes came from

The vowel shapes were not guessed. In 1952 Peterson and Barney measured the resonances of thousands of spoken vowels, and those numbers are still the standard reference. We searched over tube shapes until the simulation matched their measurements.

What came out of that search is the nice part. Nobody told it any phonetics, but it independently found that /o/ needs a narrowing at the back of the throat plus rounded lips, and that /ɑ/ needs a narrowing low in the throat with the jaw open — which is exactly what those vowels are.

Under the hood

The model is a Kelly–Lochbaum digital waveguide (1962). The tract is a chain of cylindrical sections carrying two counter-propagating travelling waves. At every boundary where the cross-sectional area changes, part of each wave reflects:

r = (A₁ − A₂) / (A₁ + A₂)

That is the entire model. There are no formant filters anywhere in the code — the resonances are standing waves, and each cylinder in the 3D view is lit by the wave energy actually present in that section, so you are watching the pressure pattern that is a formant.

The /l/ needs more than a single tube. A lateral splits the airflow around the tongue and leaves a closed pocket, which puts a zero in the transfer function — and a single unbranched tube is an all-pole system that cannot produce one. So the tract now carries a side branch, coupled through a three-port scattering junction:

pj = 2·Σu⁺ / ΣA   ·   u⁻ᵢ = Aᵢ·pj − u⁺ᵢ

which reduces exactly to the two-port form when the branch is shut, so it cannot disturb the tract it taps into. With the lateral re-solved around it, the shipping engine measures a −33.7 dB notch at 1950 Hz. The same mechanism with an open far end at the velum will give the nasals.

Still missing: nasals, voiceless stops and fricatives. The glottal source is a Rosenberg pulse, which models speech rather than shouting — replacing it with the LF model is the next real step, since a shout is pressed phonation and not merely loud speech.
